Pourous Pavement in NYC

Pourous, what?

Text in this box is from NYC Department of Design and Construction's page on Porous Pavement

"Porous pavement allows stormwater to be naturally absorbed into the ground, rather than pooling on the street or flowing into the sewer system. The pavement itself is mixed without the smaller aggregate present in traditional pavement, and liquid can easily pass through the holes in the larger aggregate. This helps to prevent flooding and reduces the chance of icy conditions forming on the road. Despite the difference in permeability, porous pavement can be walked on, parked on, or driven over without issue."

Huh?

Keledy's version
Unlike traditional pavement, that does not absorb water, Porous Pavement is made in a way to provide structure for cars and humans but also to allow water to pass through into the ground. It is as if the road was made out of sponges.


Two summers ago, the road by my apartment was being ripped up, again.
(This is the reason I picked this topic for my "Tribute Page" assignment)
I knew nothing about Pororous Pavement before the jackhammers started ripping up the road. There was scant information online and there was a posted sign with the nebulous title "Green Infrastructure Upgrades in the Gravesend Bay Area" (Sunset Park is in the Gravesend Bay Area?)
I have become increasingly curious about this new way of building roads to help with rainwater events that have been happening more frequently in my neighborhood.
I wish there was more information out there for people not in the Green Infrastructure field to learn more about Porous Pavement.

The NYC Department of Environmental Protection (DEP) is working on Green Infrastructure Upgrades to handle the effects of climate change.
One flood mitigation strategy they are testing out is installing Pororous Pavement in neighborhoods whose sewers are the aging Combined Sewer Overflows (CSO) that collect both rainwater and sewage in the same pipes. When the system is overwhelmed, like when there are heavy rainfalls, the rain and sewage get combined and are discharged into the waterway.
Yuck.
In my neighborhood during heavy rains, sometimes the water from the sewers comes up into the streets.
It is gross.
For the Porous Pavement to be installed, the construction team dug up one side of the road underneath where cars are usually parked.
On the side of the road, underneath parked cars, NYC DDC (Department of Design and Construction) dug up the road. Then they built concrete channels. They filled those channels with rocks. They installed precast pourous pavement on top to help with preventing stormwater from going to the sewers.

    Porous pavement is special roadway paving that is designed to collect and manage stormwater that runs off the streets and sidewalks when it rains.

  • Improves street drainage
  • Reduces puddles and ponds
  • Reduce pollution to New York City waterways
